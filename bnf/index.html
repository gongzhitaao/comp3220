PPL – BNF

We describe the syntax of programming languages with BNF and e-BNF.

1 Introduction

BNF, short for Backus Normal Form or Backus–Naur Form, is a metalanguage used to describe other languages, including itself. The following snippet shows one possible representation of BNF syntax by itself [1]. 

<syntax>         ::= <rule> | <rule> <syntax>
<rule>           ::= <opt-whitespace> "<" <rule-name> ">" <opt-whitespace> "::="
                     <opt-whitespace> <expression> <line-end>
<opt-whitespace> ::= " " <opt-whitespace> | ""
<expression>     ::= <list> | <list> "|" <expression>
<line-end>       ::= <opt-whitespace> <EOL> | <line-end> <line-end>
<list>           ::= <term> | <term> <opt-whitespace> <list>
<term>           ::= <literal> | "<" <rule-name> ">"
<literal>        ::= '"' <text> '"' | "'" <text> "'"

BNF represention, as seen above, is actually a collection of derivation rules, where the left-hand-side (LHS) may be replaced by the right-hand-side (RHS). Symbols that appear on LHS are nonterminals, e.g., <syntax>, <rule> and etc. And symbols that never appears on LFS are terminals, e.g., <rule-name> and <text>. Terminal, as its name suggests, is the elementary symbols of the languages being represented. It may not be changed by the derivation rules, since it never appears on the LHS.

The symbol "|" means logic OR. A nonterminal may have multiple derivation rules, which can be written separately or joined together with "|" as seen above. The <syntax> representation can be also written as 

<syntax> ::= <rule>
<syntax> ::= <rule> <syntax>

Note that the rule may be ambiguous, which will be discussed in detail in Section 2.

1.1 Relationship with CFG

CFG, short for context-free grammar, which is a formal grammer in formal language theory. Usually is it defined by a 4-tuple, see [2]. BNF is popular notation of CFG. In other words, they are doing the same thing.
